With a marker in hand and an elongated ballot sheet in front of us, we citizens elect our fellow citizens based on the responses we've come up with to the question, "What will happen after the elections?" Though I would not debate the importance or truth of this topic, I believe we must also consider the following: "Why should we vote?" "We should vote," as a positive and frequently stated response, cannot stand up to the weight of a subject that requires additional investigation.

So, what's the point of asking the question?

Consequently, we must remember that while our votes help politicians win, they also contribute to maintain our democratic system as a whole. We are not only electing particular politicians, but also validating the government process of our country. Votes determine victory, and voter turnout establishes a political system's legitimacy. Citizens have the power to determine the state's survival, development, or extinction as members of the state (at the very least as tax payers).

Moreover, A political system can gain its legitimacy through numerical means through the election process. Though this is insufficient to assure near-absolute legitimacy (the goal for legitimate government), it is adequate to allow a system to establish internal cohesiveness (i.e., it will continue to operate as a whole) and to legitimize its existence as a power center in our country.

All in all, there 97 presidential candidates. Here, we are only going to present candidates who had served before and experienced in the field of politics. Also, in the past few months, these are the names that are mostly mentioned online.

Panfilo "Ping" Lacson

Panfilo "Ping" Morena Lacson Sr. is a Filipino politician and former police general serving as a Senator since early 2016, and previously from 2001 to 2013. He was also the Director General of the Philippine National Police from 1999 to 2001, and was a candidate in the 2004 presidential election.

Maria Leonor Leni Robredo

Maria Leonor "Leni" Gerona Robredo is a Filipina lawyer, politician, and social activist who is the 14th and incumbent vice president of the Philippines. Robredo was the wife of the late Interior Secretary Jesse Robredo. She served as the representative of Camarines Sur's 3rd district from 2013 until her inauguration as vice president in 2016. In the vice presidential election, Robredo defeated Bongbong Marcos by a narrow margin of 263,473 votes
